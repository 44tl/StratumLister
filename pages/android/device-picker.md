# Android Device Picker

Pick Android hardware by update support, repair path, bootloader policy, and app compatibility before camera specs or benchmark numbers.

## Quick Picks

| Use case | Pick by | Why |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Normal daily phone | Long official OS and security support | Updates matter more than small spec differences. |
| Privacy-focused daily phone | Supported Pixel with a well-documented privacy OS option | Pixels have clear factory images, long update windows, and strong custom OS support. |
| Root learning device | Unlockable spare device with factory images available | Mistakes wipe data and can soft-brick the phone. Do not learn on your main phone. |
| Custom ROM device | Official support page from the ROM project | A forum build with no maintainer is not the same as an officially supported build. |
| Repair/longevity focus | Published parts and support roadmap | Batteries, screens, and charging ports decide whether a phone survives years of use. |
| Business or managed fleet | Android Enterprise Recommended requirements | The baseline expects security updates for five years from initial ship date. |

## Update Support Rules

- Pixel 8 and later phones are listed by Google for seven years of OS and security updates from first US Google Store availability.
- Pixel 6, Pixel 7, Pixel 7a, Pixel Fold, and Pixel 6a are listed by Google for five years of OS and security updates.
- Android Enterprise Recommended device requirements include five years of security update support and security updates made available within a 90-day window.
- Some manufacturers publish strong update promises, but check the exact model and region. Carrier and regional variants can differ.
- Do not buy a phone whose final security patch is already near or past its end date for banking, work, or primary accounts.

## Bootloader Questions

Before buying for root or ROM use, confirm:

- The exact model number and codename.
- Whether OEM unlocking is available in Developer options.
- Whether the carrier variant blocks unlocking.
- Whether factory images or full OTA packages are available.
- Whether the ROM you want supports that exact model.
- Whether the ROM supports relocking the bootloader after install.

If any answer is unclear, assume the device is a bad root or ROM target.

## Used Phone Checklist

- Check current Android version, security patch level, and Google Play system update.
- Confirm the device is not carrier locked if that matters to you.
- Confirm OEM unlocking status before buying for root or custom ROM work.
- Check battery health using manufacturer tools when available.
- Confirm the cameras, speakers, microphone, cellular bands, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, and USB port work.
- Factory reset only after the seller removes their Google account and screen lock.
- Avoid phones with unknown repair history, missing IMEI labels, or suspiciously cheap pricing.

## What To Avoid

- Old flagships that look cheap but no longer receive patches.
- Imported variants with different bands, bootloader rules, or update channels.
- Phones sold as "global ROM" when the device was not actually intended for your region.
- Devices where unlocking requires paid gray-market tools.
- Devices where the only ROM builds are abandoned Telegram uploads.
